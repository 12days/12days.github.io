What is 12 days of coding?

It is 12 projects in 12 days. Each project would be started, finished, and shipped in a given October day.

It is an experiment in coding and learning. It is an experiment in teaming and creating a culture of doing.

I will be blogging about our experience and lessons in [12-days-blog](http://12days.blogspot.com/).

Why 12 days of coding?

Josh and I both had time to kill. But, not too much. We wanted to do something fun, build up our coding skills, and our portfolios.

And we wanted to learn: learn to be better coders, learn to be better builders, learn more about our interests. And that was it. We didn't want to get ahead of ourselves.

We thought a good way to do this would be to create a set of constraints around our efforts. Constraints are key to discipline and focus.

  • One of the first choices we made was to keep it short and simple (KISS principle).
  • We are devoting around 2-weeks to this experiment.
  • We are keeping this to 2-person engineering team.
  • We are doing projects we can reasonably finish in a day.

Overview: our values and methodology

To be effective, you have to have a process. The hero is neither the knowledge nor the superstar coder. The hero is the process. We want to learn to be better at a successful-building-process by doing.

Values

We'd brainstorm a one sentence idea, then share them with each other after 5-minutes. We did a few of these 5-minute sprints. We then scored them using four criteria. The criteria are, in essence, team values:

  • Interesting - are they interesting to us?
  • Simplicity - are they simple enough (for us) to tackle?
  • Uniqueness - are these ideas unique to us and to others?
  • Utility - would we use them?

Yes, they are arbitrary. Yes, they are imperfect set of categories. We can live with that. Can you?

Methodology

What about the process? No ground-breaking principles here:

  • Build on strength - for speed, use familiar technology (for us, mostly Ruby on Rails)
  • Prepare - for new technologies (e.g. a Node app), do some advance reading/tinkering
  • DRY - Do not repeat yourself - look for gems wherever possible
  • Best practices - we will use TDD; red-green-refactor; frequent commits

  • Modularity - we will try to build templates we can reuse on subsequent days
